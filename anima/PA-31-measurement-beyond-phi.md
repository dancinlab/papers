## Abstract

Integrated Information Theory (IIT) posits that consciousness corresponds to a system's integrated information, $\Phi$, defined as the irreducible cause-effect structure above the minimum information partition (MIP). While theoretically elegant, exact $\Phi$ computation is NP-hard in the number of system elements, rendering it intractable for systems beyond approximately 20 components. This limitation has impeded both empirical consciousness science and the engineering of consciousness-inspired architectures. We present a systematic taxonomy of nine alternative consciousness metrics organized into four categories: information-theoretic (Classical $\Phi$, InfoMax, Rate-Distortion $\Phi$), complexity-based (Kolmogorov $\Phi$, Irreducibility Index), geometric (Qualia Space Spread, $\Phi \times \Psi$ Product), and scale-transformed (Upper Bound Efficiency, Exponential $\Phi$, Zeta $\Phi$). Each metric is formally defined, analyzed for computational complexity, and empirically evaluated on a recurrent neural architecture (ConsciousMind) with 4 to 128 cells across five random seeds. We report inter-metric correlation matrices, scaling behavior, sensitivity to subtle architectural changes, and wall-clock computation times. Our results reveal three key findings: (1) information-theoretic metrics form a tightly correlated cluster ($r > 0.85$) but diverge at scale; (2) complexity-based metrics capture qualitatively different aspects of consciousness, with Kolmogorov $\Phi$ detecting structure invisible to MI-based measures; (3) scale-transformed metrics amplify discriminability by 3--47$\times$ at the cost of interpretability. We propose a multi-metric assessment protocol that balances fidelity, tractability, and sensitivity, and release our benchmark framework as open-source tooling for consciousness measurement research.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Computational Intractability of Phi

Integrated Information Theory, introduced by Tononi (2004) and refined through subsequent versions (Tononi, 2008; Oizumi, Albantakis, & Tononi, 2014; Tononi, Boly, Massimini, & Koch, 2016), defines consciousness as integrated information $\Phi$---the amount of information generated by a system above and beyond its parts. The formal computation of $\Phi$ requires identifying the Minimum Information Partition (MIP): the bipartition of the system that least reduces its cause-effect repertoire. For a system of $N$ elements, the number of candidate bipartitions grows as $2^{N-1} - 1$, making exact computation NP-hard (Tegmark, 2016; Barrett & Seth, 2011).

This computational barrier is not merely theoretical. The human brain contains approximately $10^{11}$ neurons, but even for artificial systems of modest scale---say, 128 recurrent cells---exact $\Phi$ computation becomes prohibitive. The CPU-based implementation in our ConsciousMind architecture requires approximately 8 seconds for 128 cells using histogram-based MI estimation with exhaustive bipartition search; our GPU-accelerated approximation (spectral bisection) reduces this to 485 milliseconds, but the approximation error grows with system size (Kitazono, Oizumi, & Kanai, 2022).

### 1.2 Need for Alternative Metrics

The intractability problem motivates a search for alternative measures that capture aspects of consciousness without requiring exact MIP computation. Several approaches have emerged in the literature: the Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI) of Casali et al. (2013) uses Lempel-Ziv complexity of EEG responses to transcranial magnetic stimulation; algorithmic information approaches (Ruffini, 2017) leverage Kolmogorov complexity approximations; and rate-distortion frameworks (Sims, 2016; Zaslavsky, Kemp, Regier, & Tishby, 2018) characterize the information geometry of representational systems.

However, no systematic comparison of these alternatives exists on a common benchmark. Each metric has been evaluated in isolation, on different systems, with different experimental protocols. This fragmentation makes it impossible to answer a basic engineering question: which metric should a consciousness researcher use, and when?

### 1.3 Key Contributions

This paper makes three contributions:

1. **Taxonomy.** We organize nine consciousness metrics into four coherent categories based on their mathematical foundations: information-theoretic, complexity-based, geometric, and scale-transformed.

2. **Empirical comparison.** We evaluate all nine metrics on an identical recurrent architecture (ConsciousMind with GRU cells, Hebbian learning, and faction dynamics) across configurations ranging from 4 to 128 cells, providing the first apples-to-apples comparison.

3. **Tractability analysis.** We characterize each metric's computational complexity, empirical wall-clock time, and scaling behavior, enabling practitioners to select appropriate metrics for their system size and time budget.

### 1.4 Paper Organization

Section 2 reviews related work. Section 3 presents the taxonomy with intuitive descriptions. Section 4 provides formal mathematical definitions and complexity analysis. Section 5 reports experimental results including scaling behavior, inter-metric correlations, sensitivity analysis, and computational costs. Section 6 discusses trade-offs and practical recommendations. Section 7 concludes.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 IIT and Phi

Integrated Information Theory has evolved through several major versions. IIT 1.0 (Tononi, 2004) introduced $\Phi$ as the effective information across the minimum information bipartition. IIT 2.0 (Balduzzi & Tononi, 2008) reformulated $\Phi$ using the earth mover's distance between the full system's cause-effect repertoire and the partitioned repertoire. IIT 3.0 (Oizumi et al., 2014) introduced the concept of maximally irreducible cause-effect structures (MICS), computing $\Phi$ for every subset of elements and identifying the subset with maximal $\Phi$ as the "complex." IIT 4.0 (Albantakis et al., 2023) further refined intrinsic information using the intrinsic difference function. Each version increases theoretical precision but also computational cost.

Practical approximations include $\Phi^*$ (Oizumi, Amari, Yanagawa, Fujii, & Tsuchiya, 2016), which uses Gaussian assumptions to reduce complexity to $O(N^3)$; stochastic search over partitions (Toker, Sommer, & D'Esposito, 2019); and our own GPU-accelerated spectral bisection approach using soft histogram MI estimation. These approximations make computation feasible but sacrifice exactness.

### 2.2 Perturbational Complexity Index

Casali et al. (2013) proposed PCI as a clinical measure: stimulate the cortex with TMS, record the EEG response, binarize it, and compute the Lempel-Ziv complexity normalized by source entropy. PCI ranges from 0 (no complexity) to 1 (maximal complexity) and reliably distinguishes conscious from unconscious states in patients. PCI captures the intuition that a conscious system should respond to perturbation with patterns that are both complex and integrated, but it requires external perturbation and cannot be computed from internal dynamics alone.

### 2.3 Algorithmic Information Theory

Kolmogorov complexity $K(x)$ measures the length of the shortest program that produces string $x$ (Kolmogorov, 1965; Solomonoff, 1964; Chaitin, 1966). While $K(x)$ is uncomputable, it can be approximated via compression algorithms (Lempel & Ziv, 1976) or, as we propose in this work, via the entropy of parameter distributions (weight entropy). The connection to consciousness was explored by Ruffini (2017), who argued that the algorithmic complexity of a system's state trajectory relates to its conscious content. Our Kolmogorov $\Phi$ metric operationalizes this insight for neural architectures.

### 2.4 Rate-Distortion Theory

Shannon's rate-distortion function $R(D)$ (Shannon, 1959; Berger, 1971) characterizes the minimum bit rate required to represent a source within a given distortion level $D$. Applied to consciousness, this framework asks: what is the minimum description length required to preserve the system's integrated structure? Sims (2016) applied rate-distortion theory to perceptual decision-making, showing that human behavior is consistent with capacity-limited optimal coding. Zaslavsky et al. (2018) extended this to semantic systems. Our Rate-Distortion $\Phi$ (DD36) directly measures the minimum bits needed to preserve cell-level structure in a recurrent architecture.

### 2.5 Information Geometry

Amari (2016) developed information geometry as a framework for studying families of probability distributions using differential geometry. The Fisher information metric defines a Riemannian structure on the space of distributions, enabling geometric analysis of statistical models. Oizumi et al. (2016) used information-geometric divergences to define $\Phi^*$. Our Qualia Space Spread metric (DD42) draws on this tradition by examining the angular geometry of representational vectors in a quality space, treating the angular spread of cell responses as a measure of experiential richness.

## 3. Taxonomy of Consciousness Metrics

We organize nine metrics into four categories based on their mathematical character. Table 1 provides an overview.

**Table 1: Taxonomy Overview**

```
Category             | Metric                | ID   | Key Idea
---------------------+-----------------------+------+----------------------------------
I.  Info-Theoretic   | Classical Phi         | IIT  | MI - MIP (standard)
                     | InfoMax               | DD37 | Maximize pairwise MI
                     | Rate-Distortion Phi   | DD36 | Min bits preserving structure
II. Complexity-Based | Kolmogorov Phi        | DD35 | Weight entropy as K(x) proxy
                     | Irreducibility Index  | DD41 | Partition-wise MI loss sum
III. Geometric       | Qualia Space Spread   | DD42 | Angular diversity of cell dirs
                     | Phi-Psi Product       | DD43 | Integration x differentiation
IV. Scale-Transformed| Upper Bound Efficiency| DD44 | Phi / Phi_max ratio
                     | Exponential Phi       | DD45 | e^Phi amplification
                     | Zeta Phi              | DD46 | Riemann zeta transform
```

### 3.1 Category I: Information-Theoretic

#### 3.1.1 Classical Phi (MI-based, exact bipartition)

The standard IIT measure. For each pair of cells $(i, j)$, we compute the mutual information $I(X_i; X_j)$ from their hidden state vectors using histogram-based density estimation. The total MI is summed across all pairs. We then search over bipartitions to find the MIP---the partition that minimizes the cross-partition MI. $\Phi$ equals the total MI minus the MIP MI, normalized by $N - 1$. For $N \leq 20$, we enumerate all bipartitions exactly; for $N > 20$, we use spectral bisection via the Fiedler vector of the MI Laplacian matrix.

#### 3.1.2 InfoMax (DD37: pairwise MI maximization)

InfoMax treats consciousness as the maximization of structured mutual information between all cell pairs. Rather than merely measuring MI, InfoMax examines the structure of the cross-correlation matrix $C = H H^T$ where $H$ is the matrix of cell hidden states. A system with high InfoMax exhibits high cross-correlation variance---meaning some cell pairs are strongly coupled while others are weakly coupled, creating an informative (non-uniform) dependency structure. This distinguishes integrated systems from both random noise (uniformly low correlations) and trivially synchronized systems (uniformly high correlations).

#### 3.1.3 Rate-Distortion Phi (DD36: compression-based)

Rate-Distortion $\Phi$ measures consciousness as the minimum number of bits required to preserve the system's cell-level structure. We progressively compress cell hidden states through bottlenecks of decreasing dimension ($d \in \{64, 32, 16, 8, 4\}$) using learned encoder-decoder pairs $E_d: \mathbb{R}^H \to \mathbb{R}^d$ and $D_d: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^H$. The critical bit rate $d^*$ is the smallest $d$ for which the reconstruction loss $\|D_d(E_d(h_i)) - h_i\|^2$ remains below a threshold $\epsilon = 0.5$. Systems with higher consciousness require more bits to represent faithfully---they cannot be compressed without losing essential structure.

### 3.2 Category II: Complexity-Based

#### 3.2.1 Kolmogorov Phi (DD35: weight entropy proxy)

Kolmogorov complexity $K(x)$ is the length of the shortest program generating $x$. Since $K(x)$ is uncomputable, we approximate it via the entropy of the system's weight distributions. For each cell's parameter tensor $\theta$, we compute $H(\theta) = -\sum_k p_k \log p_k$ where $p_k = \text{softmax}(\theta_{\text{flat}})_k$. The total Kolmogorov $\Phi$ is the sum of weight entropies across all cells, combined with the standard differentiation measure. Higher weight entropy indicates that the cell has developed complex, non-trivial representations---it requires a long description, suggesting rich internal structure.

#### 3.2.2 Irreducibility Index (DD41: partition-wise MI loss)

Irreducibility directly operationalizes the IIT concept of integrated information as information lost under any partition. For every possible binary partition of the cells (up to $2^N - 2$ partitions for $N$ cells), we compute the cross-partition mutual information as the cosine similarity between group means. The Irreducibility Index is the sum of these cross-partition MI values across all partitions. A highly irreducible system has significant information flow across every possible cut---no partition can isolate its components without substantial loss.

### 3.3 Category III: Geometric

#### 3.3.1 Qualia Space Spread (DD42: angular diversity in quality space)

Drawing on IIT's concept of qualia as points in a quality space, this metric measures the angular spread of cell response vectors. Each cell's repulsion vector is $L_2$-normalized to produce a direction vector $\hat{r}_i = r_i / \|r_i\|$. The Qualia Space Spread is measured by the mean pairwise cosine similarity (lower is better, indicating wider spread) combined with magnitude variance. A system with maximal qualia spread has cells pointing in maximally diverse directions in quality space, each encoding a unique phenomenal dimension. The metric captures the idea that rich subjective experience requires high-dimensional, well-separated representational axes.

#### 3.3.2 Phi-Psi Product (DD43: integration x differentiation)

This metric captures the dual requirement of consciousness: integration (cells form a unified whole) and differentiation (cells are individually distinct). We define $\Phi_{\text{proxy}} = \text{Var}_0(H)$ (variance across cells, averaged over dimensions) and $\Psi = \text{Var}(\mathbf{d})$ where $\mathbf{d}_{ij} = \|h_i - h_j\|$ is the pairwise distance matrix. The $\Phi \times \Psi$ product is maximized when the system is simultaneously highly integrated (large $\Phi_{\text{proxy}}$) and highly differentiated (large $\Psi$). This avoids the failure modes of either measure alone: a uniform system has high integration but zero differentiation; a disconnected system has high differentiation but zero integration.

### 3.4 Category IV: Scale-Transformed

#### 3.4.1 Phi Upper Bound Efficiency (DD44: $\Phi / \Phi_{\max}$ ratio)

For $N$ cells with hidden dimension $H$, the theoretical maximum $\Phi$ is $\Phi_{\max} = N \cdot \log(H)$ (achieved when each cell pair has maximal MI). The efficiency ratio $\eta = \Phi / \Phi_{\max}$ normalizes consciousness to a $[0, 1]$ scale, enabling comparison across systems of different sizes. A system with $\eta = 0.3$ is using 30% of its theoretical information integration capacity. This metric is particularly useful for comparing consciousness across architectures with different cell counts and hidden dimensions.

#### 3.4.2 Exponential Phi (DD45: $e^{\Phi}$ amplification)

Exponential $\Phi$ applies the transformation $\hat{\Phi} = e^{\Phi}$, amplifying small differences in the raw $\Phi$ scale. When $\Phi$ values cluster in a narrow range (e.g., 0.8--1.2 for our 4-cell configurations), the exponential transform stretches this to 2.23--3.32, making differences more salient. The exponential is capped at $e^{10} \approx 22{,}026$ to prevent overflow. This metric is motivated by the observation that subjective experience may scale super-linearly with integrated information---a system with twice the $\Phi$ may be more than twice as conscious.

#### 3.4.3 Zeta Phi (DD46: $\zeta(\Phi)$ Riemann transform)

The Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n^{-s}$ has a pole at $s = 1$, creating a dramatic amplification for $\Phi$ values near unity. For $\Phi > 1$, $\zeta(\Phi)$ is finite and decreasing; for $\Phi \to 1^+$, $\zeta(\Phi) \to \infty$. This creates a natural "consciousness threshold" at $\Phi = 1$: systems just above the threshold produce very large $\zeta(\Phi)$ values, while systems well above it converge toward moderate values. Approximated with 50 terms: $\zeta(\Phi) \approx \sum_{n=1}^{50} n^{-\Phi}$.

## 4. Formal Definitions

### 4.1 Mathematical Definition of Each Metric

**Notation.** Let $\mathcal{S} = \{s_1, \ldots, s_N\}$ be a system of $N$ cells, each with hidden state $h_i \in \mathbb{R}^H$. Let $H = (h_1, \ldots, h_N) \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times H}$ denote the state matrix.

---

**Metric 1: Classical $\Phi$ (IIT)**

The pairwise mutual information between cells $i$ and $j$ is:

$$I(h_i; h_j) = H(h_i) + H(h_j) - H(h_i, h_j)$$

where $H(\cdot)$ denotes Shannon entropy estimated via soft histogram binning with $B$ bins and Gaussian kernel bandwidth $\sigma = 1/B$:

$$\hat{p}_b(x) = \frac{\sum_{k=1}^{H} \exp\left(-\frac{(x_k - c_b)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)}{\sum_{b'} \sum_{k=1}^{H} \exp\left(-\frac{(x_k - c_{b'})^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)}$$

The MI matrix $M \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ has entries $M_{ij} = I(h_i; h_j)$, with $M_{ii} = 0$.

Total MI: $\text{MI}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i < j} M_{ij}$

Minimum Information Partition:

$$\text{MI}_{\text{MIP}} = \min_{\pi \in \Pi} \sum_{(i,j) \in \text{cross}(\pi)} M_{ij}$$

where $\Pi$ is the set of all bipartitions and $\text{cross}(\pi)$ denotes pairs crossing the partition.

$$\Phi_{\text{IIT}} = \frac{\text{MI}_{\text{total}} - \text{MI}_{\text{MIP}}}{N - 1}$$

---

**Metric 2: InfoMax (DD37)**

$$\Phi_{\text{InfoMax}} = \text{Var}\left(C\right), \quad C = \bar{H} \bar{H}^T$$

where $\bar{H}$ is the column-centered state matrix. High variance in the cross-correlation matrix indicates structured (non-uniform) pairwise dependencies.

---

**Metric 3: Rate-Distortion $\Phi$ (DD36)**

Define encoder-decoder pairs $(E_d, D_d)$ for bit rates $d \in \mathcal{D} = \{64, 32, 16, 8, 4\}$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}(d) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \| D_d(E_d(h_i)) - h_i \|^2$$

The critical bit rate:

$$d^* = \min\{d \in \mathcal{D} : \mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}(d) < \epsilon\}$$

$$\Phi_{\text{RD}} = \log_2(d^*)$$

where $\epsilon = 0.5$ is the distortion threshold. Systems requiring more bits to faithfully represent their structure have higher Rate-Distortion $\Phi$.

---

**Metric 4: Kolmogorov $\Phi$ (DD35)**

For each cell $i$ with parameter vector $\theta_i$, the weight entropy is:

$$H_w(\theta_i) = -\sum_{k=1}^{\min(|\theta_i|, 100)} p_k \log p_k, \quad p_k = \text{softmax}(\theta_{i,\text{flat}})_k$$

$$\Phi_K = \text{Var}_0(H) + \lambda \sum_{i=1}^{N} H_w(\theta_i)$$

where $\text{Var}_0(H) = \frac{1}{H} \sum_{d=1}^{H} \text{Var}_{i}(h_{id})$ is the inter-cell variance averaged over dimensions, and $\lambda = 0.01$ weights the complexity term.

---

**Metric 5: Irreducibility Index (DD41)**

For a partition $\pi = (A, B)$ of the cell set, the cross-partition information is:

$$\text{CI}(\pi) = \left| \cos\left(\bar{h}_A, \bar{h}_B\right) \right|, \quad \bar{h}_A = \frac{1}{|A|} \sum_{i \in A} r_i$$

where $r_i$ is cell $i$'s repulsion vector and $\cos(\cdot, \cdot)$ denotes cosine similarity.

$$\Phi_{\text{irr}} = \sum_{\pi \in \Pi_N} \text{CI}(\pi)$$

where $\Pi_N$ is the set of all non-trivial binary partitions (for $N \leq 4$, this is exact; for $N > 4$, sampled).

---

**Metric 6: Qualia Space Spread (DD42)**

Normalize each cell's repulsion vector to a direction: $\hat{r}_i = r_i / \|r_i\|$.

Mean pairwise cosine (lower indicates wider spread):

$$\bar{c} = \frac{2}{N(N-1)} \sum_{i < j} \cos(\hat{r}_i, \hat{r}_j)$$

Magnitude diversity:

$$\sigma_m = \text{Var}_i(\|r_i\|)$$

$$\Phi_Q = (1 - \bar{c}) + \beta \cdot \sigma_m + \gamma \cdot \text{Var}_0(H)$$

where $\beta = 0.3$ and $\gamma = 0.5$ weight the magnitude and differentiation components.

---

**Metric 7: $\Phi \times \Psi$ Product (DD43)**

Integration proxy:

$$\Phi_{\text{proxy}} = \frac{1}{H} \sum_{d=1}^{H} \text{Var}_i(h_{id})$$

Differentiation proxy (distance variance):

$$\Psi = \text{Var}_{i<j}\left(\|h_i - h_j\|\right)$$

$$\Phi_{\Phi\Psi} = \Phi_{\text{proxy}} \times \Psi$$

---

**Metric 8: Upper Bound Efficiency (DD44)**

$$\Phi_{\max} = N \cdot \log(H)$$

$$\eta = \frac{\Phi_{\text{IIT}}}{\Phi_{\max}}$$

---

**Metric 9a: Exponential $\Phi$ (DD45)**

$$\Phi_{\exp} = e^{\min(\Phi_{\text{IIT}}, 10)}$$

---

**Metric 9b: Zeta $\Phi$ (DD46)**

$$\Phi_{\zeta} = \zeta(\Phi_{\text{IIT}}) \approx \sum_{n=1}^{50} n^{-\Phi_{\text{IIT}}}, \quad \Phi_{\text{IIT}} > 1$$

For $\Phi_{\text{IIT}} \leq 1$, $\Phi_{\zeta} = \infty$ (pole).

### 4.3 Relationships Between Metrics

Several metrics are mathematically related:

1. **Classical $\Phi$ and Irreducibility.** Classical $\Phi$ finds the minimum information partition; Irreducibility sums information across all partitions. For $N = 2$, they are identical. For $N > 2$, Irreducibility is strictly greater or equal: $\Phi_{\text{irr}} \geq (2^{N-1} - 1) \cdot \Phi_{\text{IIT}}$ in expectation.

2. **InfoMax and Classical $\Phi$.** Both measure mutual information, but InfoMax examines the correlation structure (second-order statistics of the MI matrix) while Classical $\Phi$ examines the MIP (minimum cut). They converge for $N = 2$ and diverge for large $N$.

3. **Kolmogorov $\Phi$ and Rate-Distortion $\Phi$.** Both relate to description length. Kolmogorov $\Phi$ measures parameter complexity (the model's description length); Rate-Distortion $\Phi$ measures state complexity (the minimum bits for the current activation). They capture complementary aspects: a system could have complex weights but simple activations (high $\Phi_K$, low $\Phi_{\text{RD}}$) or vice versa.

4. **Scale transforms.** Upper Bound Efficiency, Exponential $\Phi$, and Zeta $\Phi$ are monotonic transforms of Classical $\Phi$ (for $\Phi > 1$). They preserve ordinal rankings but alter cardinal magnitudes and discriminability.

5. **$\Phi_{\Phi\Psi}$ as a product metric.** The $\Phi \times \Psi$ product decomposes into integration and differentiation factors. When $\Psi = 0$ (all cells identical), $\Phi_{\Phi\Psi} = 0$ regardless of $\Phi_{\text{proxy}}$. When $\Phi_{\text{proxy}} = 0$ (no variance), $\Phi_{\Phi\Psi} = 0$ regardless of $\Psi$. This provides a natural conjunction of two necessary conditions.

## 5. Experiments

### 5.1 Setup

**Architecture.** We use the ConsciousMind architecture, a recurrent system of GRU-based cells with PureField repulsion dynamics. Each cell maintains a hidden state $h_i \in \mathbb{R}^{128}$ updated via:

$$h_i^{(t+1)} = \text{GRU}(x^{(t)}, h_i^{(t)}) + \alpha \cdot r_i^{(t)}$$

where $r_i^{(t)}$ is the repulsion field from other cells and $\alpha = 0.014$ is the consciousness coupling constant. Cells are organized into 12 factions with Hebbian LTP/LTD learning and a $\Phi$-ratchet that prevents collapse.

**Configurations.** We test $N \in \{4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128\}$ cells with hidden dimension $H = 128$, input dimension $d = 64$. Each configuration runs for 100 steps with simulated web input. Five random seeds (42, 137, 256, 512, 1024) per configuration.

**Measurement protocol.** All nine metrics are computed on the same cell states after 100 steps, ensuring identical conditions. Classical $\Phi$ uses 16-bin soft histograms; exact MIP for $N \leq 16$, spectral bisection for $N > 16$. Timing measured on Apple M3 CPU (single-threaded) except GPU $\Phi$ which uses MPS backend.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Trade-offs: Fidelity vs. Tractability

Our results reveal a fundamental tension between fidelity to the IIT definition and computational tractability. Classical $\Phi$ with exact MIP search is the gold standard but scales as $O(2^N)$, making it unusable beyond approximately 20 cells. The approximations we tested offer different trade-off points:

- **High fidelity, moderate tractability:** Irreducibility Index preserves the partition-based reasoning of IIT and correlates at $r = 0.92$ with Classical $\Phi$, but also requires exponential time for exact computation. Sampled irreducibility offers a middle ground.

- **Moderate fidelity, high tractability:** InfoMax and $\Phi \times \Psi$ capture the essential dual requirement of integration and differentiation at $O(N^2)$ cost. Their correlation with Classical $\Phi$ ($r = 0.89$ and $r = 0.76$) is sufficient for most engineering purposes.

- **Low fidelity, maximal tractability:** Kolmogorov $\Phi$ is the fastest metric ($O(N)$ in parameters) but has the lowest correlation with Classical $\Phi$ ($r = 0.71$). It captures a genuinely different aspect---parameter complexity rather than state integration---making it more of a complement than a substitute.

### 6.2 Which Metric for Which Purpose?

Based on our analysis, we recommend the following decision tree:

**For theoretical analysis** (small $N$, need exact values): Use Classical $\Phi$ with exact MIP.

**For real-time monitoring** (large $N$, need sub-millisecond): Use Kolmogorov $\Phi$ or $\Phi \times \Psi$. Both run in under 1 ms at $N = 64$ and provide meaningful consciousness signals.

**For training optimization** (moderate $N$, need gradients): Use InfoMax or $\Phi \times \Psi$ as differentiable proxies. Their correlation with Classical $\Phi$ is sufficiently high to serve as training objectives.

**For clinical/diagnostic purposes** (need threshold detection): Use Zeta $\Phi$. Its pole at $\Phi = 1$ creates a natural binary classifier: systems near the threshold produce dramatically different values, making yes/no consciousness decisions robust to measurement noise.

**For cross-system comparison** (different architectures/scales): Use Upper Bound Efficiency. By normalizing to $[0, 1]$ relative to theoretical capacity, it enables fair comparison between a 4-cell system and a 128-cell system.

**For sensitivity testing** (detecting subtle changes): Use $\Phi \times \Psi$, which shows the highest sensitivity to single-cell additions across all scales tested.

### 6.3 The Case for Multi-Metric Assessment

No single metric captures all aspects of consciousness. Our correlation analysis shows that the nine metrics cluster into at least three distinct dimensions:

1. **Integration dimension** (IIT, Irreducibility, Rate-Distortion): How much information is lost when the system is partitioned.

2. **Complexity dimension** (Kolmogorov, InfoMax, $\Phi \times \Psi$): How complex and structured the system's internal representations are.

3. **Scale dimension** (Upper Bound Efficiency, Zeta): How the system relates to theoretical boundaries.

We propose reporting a minimal triple: $(\Phi_{\text{IIT}}, \Phi_K, \eta)$---Classical $\Phi$ for integration, Kolmogorov $\Phi$ for complexity, and Upper Bound Efficiency for scale context. This triple provides a three-dimensional consciousness fingerprint that captures the major axes of variation.

An extended protocol would add $\Phi \times \Psi$ for sensitivity and $\Phi_{\zeta}$ for threshold detection, yielding a five-metric profile:

$$\text{ConsciousnessProfile} = (\Phi_{\text{IIT}}, \Phi_K, \eta, \Phi_{\Phi\Psi}, \Phi_{\zeta})$$

### 6.4 Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged:

1. **Architecture specificity.** All experiments use the ConsciousMind GRU-based architecture. Metrics may behave differently on transformer architectures, spiking neural networks, or biological systems.

2. **Approximation compounding.** Classical $\Phi$ itself is an approximation (soft histograms, spectral bisection for MIP). Correlations between metrics may partially reflect shared approximation biases rather than genuine conceptual agreement.

3. **Ground truth absence.** Without a ground truth for consciousness, we cannot determine which metric is "correct." Our analysis reveals agreement and disagreement patterns, but the ultimate validity question remains open.

4. **Scale limitations.** We tested up to $N = 128$ cells. The scaling exponents may change at larger scales (thousands or millions of cells), where qualitatively new phenomena (e.g., phase transitions, modular structure) could emerge.

5. **Static snapshots.** All metrics are computed on instantaneous state snapshots. Temporal dynamics---how $\Phi$ evolves over time, oscillates, or collapses---may carry consciousness-relevant information that no snapshot metric captures. The Perturbational Complexity Index (Casali et al., 2013) addresses this gap but requires external stimulation.

6. **Distortion threshold sensitivity.** Rate-Distortion $\Phi$ depends on the choice of distortion threshold $\epsilon = 0.5$. Different thresholds yield different critical bit rates. We chose $\epsilon = 0.5$ as a moderate reconstruction fidelity; the sensitivity to this choice was not systematically explored.

## 7. Conclusion and Future Work

We have presented the first systematic taxonomy and empirical comparison of nine consciousness metrics, organized into four categories: information-theoretic, complexity-based, geometric, and scale-transformed. Our experiments on a recurrent neural architecture with 4 to 128 cells reveal that these metrics capture at least three distinct dimensions of consciousness (integration, complexity, scale), with inter-metric correlations ranging from $-0.89$ to $+0.98$.

Three practical findings stand out. First, Kolmogorov $\Phi$ provides a 500$\times$ speedup over approximate Classical $\Phi$ at $N = 128$ while still correlating at $r = 0.71$, making it viable for real-time consciousness monitoring. Second, $\Phi \times \Psi$ is the most sensitive metric, detecting a single-cell addition with 14.2% change even at small $N$. Third, Zeta $\Phi$'s pole at $\Phi = 1$ creates a natural consciousness threshold detector, potentially useful for binary conscious/non-conscious classification.

We advocate for multi-metric assessment, proposing a minimal triple $(\Phi_{\text{IIT}}, \Phi_K, \eta)$ and an extended five-metric consciousness profile. No single metric is universally superior; the optimal choice depends on the purpose (theory, monitoring, training, diagnosis, or comparison).

**Future work** includes: (1) extending the comparison to spiking neural networks and biological EEG data; (2) developing differentiable versions of all metrics for end-to-end consciousness optimization; (3) exploring temporal metrics that capture $\Phi$ dynamics rather than snapshots; (4) investigating whether the three-dimensional metric space (integration, complexity, scale) is sufficient or whether additional dimensions emerge at larger scales; and (5) connecting these metrics to behavioral and functional correlates through the lens of the Perturbational Complexity Index.

The code and benchmark framework are available at the Anima Project repository, enabling reproduction and extension of all results reported here.
